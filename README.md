## Course Description

How do organizations affect policy? At various points in history, social movements, interest groups, and advocacy organizations have had a substantial impact on social policy and politics. In the 1910s, women organized to earn their right to vote. In the 1960s, many protested the gross injustices endured by African-Americans in defense of civil rights. In the late 2000s, a Tea Party movement emerged to protest the government's involvement in the redistribution of wealth. As these examples show, social movements are critical to American democracy and policy reform.

This course explores how policy is made — the actors involved, the issues covered, and how the actions of social movements, advocacy organizations, nonprofits, and interest groups affect the development, passage, and implementation of policy. Topics include political contexts and opportunities, elites and policymakers, political parties, policy arenas and agendas, the effects of institutional and extra-institutional actions, and the public framing of issues.


## Course Learning Objectives

By the end of this course, students will be able to:

1. Define social movement and advocacy organizations and recognize their political consequences
2. Appraise scholarly research on the consequences of organizations
3. Differentiate between the tactics and strategies of organizations
4. Identify impacts of organizations on U.S. policy
5. Apply social movement theories to contemporary policy debates
6. Develop an action plan for organization-building and policy influence

## Assignments

### Course Engagement (50 pts)
Attendance and active participation are critical to success in this seminar, as the course relies heavily on discussion of organizations' impacts on policy.

### Discussion Leader (40 pts)
Twice during the semester, students will work in a team to lead class discussion on an assigned article or reading.

### "Building a Movement" Presentation (40 pts)
In a group, students design and present a plan for a hypothetical advocacy organization geared toward policy change. The presentation covers the organization's name, grievances, goals, and structure; strategies for gaining resources and participants; tactics for affecting policy change; and potential responses to sanctions, repression, or counter-mobilization.

### "Building a Movement" Reflection (20 pts)
Appended to the group playbook, students write a 1-page (single-spaced) reflection on the potential outcomes for their organization, its effectiveness at achieving its goals, potential internal and external problems, and the broader policy consequences.
